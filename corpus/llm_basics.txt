Large Language Models (LLMs) are neural networks trained on huge text datasets to predict the next token in a sequence. They learn patterns of language, facts, and some reasoning abilities from this data.

Most modern LLMs use the transformer architecture. Transformers rely on self-attention, which lets the model look at all tokens in a sentence at once and decide which ones are important for predicting the next token.

An LLM represents text as vectors called embeddings. Tokenization converts text into tokens, embeddings map tokens to vectors, and attention layers update these vectors using context. After many layers, a decoder layer chooses the most likely next token step by step.

LLMs can do many tasks without task-specific training: answering questions, summarizing, translating, writing code, and more. With retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), an LLM can read external documents and use them as context to produce more accurate and grounded answers.
